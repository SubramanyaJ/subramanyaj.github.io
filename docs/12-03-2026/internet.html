Musings on the Internet
subramanyaj.github.io

For something that is going to be the pilot post, I hope this doesn't come out as a convoluted rant. Even though I'm just 20, I still remember the days the internet used to be a happier and simpler place (Perhaps it was my life that was happier and simpler).

The first computer I had the pleasure of using was an IBM ThinkCenter PC with Intel Pentium 4, when I was around 5 years old. This relic with Windows XP ran well for around 4 years, and since at that age I did not know anything about computers, I do not remember what its specifications were. Thankfully I remember the stickers that said "intel inside!" and "pentium", so after digging around, it appears that these were the specs:

  • Intel Pentium 4 @2.4GHz
  • 160 GB HDD
  • 512 MB DDR 1 RAM

At this point in time I had absolutely no idea what the internet was. Even though I noticed an "Internet Explorer" on the desktop, I could never really see what it did. And thus, my usage of the computer was limited to watching Tom and Jerry from CDs and playing some of the games my benevolent seniors who knew to use this complex machine so generously installed on it. Looking back, I can probably write those simple games myself in a few days.

The closest thing to networking we had was Bluetooth, which I vividly remember everyone using to exchange songs. Back then, possessing a 144p video of Yuvraj Singh thrashing Stuart Broad for six sixes in an over would mean that you would spend the next few weeks sharing it with everyone. Can kids these days imagine not moving out of a 5 meter range so that the transfer can complete? Thankfully, Bluetooth has still been enshrined in its deserved place with Bluetooth earpieces becoming commonplace.

I did not use another device till November 2017, when I got my hands on my faithful Dell Latitude E6420, which is the laptop that has served me the longest. However, its specifications fall short of the modern day's requirements:

  • Intel i5-2520M (Dual core) @2.5GHz
  • 512 GB HDD
  • 4 GB DDR 3 RAM

At this point I could use the internet but the network plan was such that I could not exceed 1.5 GB of usage in one day. I once wanted to install a 2 GB file, and had to preserve usage on one day, start the download at 11:30 PM on said day, then successfully continue the download into the next day since the limit reset at 12:00 AM.

By September 2023 I learned how to use Linux distributions, and this extended the laptop's lifespan to well beyond what is reasonable. After hopping distros a bazillion times, I came to the conclusion that Debian is the best for my needs (This can be a post of its own). Even though it currently sits mothballed, I've brought it back on occasion to compile OpenWrt and the ns3 simulator, among other things.

It was after 2022, that I got proper internet access, that I feel that things began to get distorted.

As soon as I entered college, I realised that modern social media has become the dominant way that people explore new circles. It has changed the way people communicate, and I'd argue that it is for the worse. It is, by design, a tool that prevents its users from leaving, while pressuring every aspiring club, business, artist, Tom, Dick and Harry to use it.

Apart from becoming the de facto method of communication engagement on the internet amongst my peers, it has somehow managed to make the technology-averse older generations to use it without complaint.

Ads are the main source of revenue for these platforms, and as someone who rarely ever makes purchases online, I find it difficult to wrap my head around this fact. And that is exactly what makes these platforms insufferable to me. Despite not using any mainstream social media regularly, I feel that the proportion of ads to actual content has been rising for quite some time now. You never can be sure that whatever you are watching doesn't turn into an advert 30 seconds in.

The most dangerous factor these platforms bring in to the equation is the sheer amount of misinformation and brainrot that they have unleashed upon the masses. Both of these have been amplified to a titanic degree by the advent of "Artificial Intelligence", which I daresay has ushered the modern world in a golden age of dopamine hits.

Of course I had to talk about LLMs, didn't I. The rampant use of LLMs has caused a net decrease in creativity. Mine, at least. My vocabulary has reduced in range, and I now even need to avoid some words that naturally occur to me just because they are very commonly used by LLMs as well. Even though I limit my LLM usage to spellchecks and reviews, it still feels like a safety net that makes me more complacent. I've been working on this for quite some time, and I cannot help but feel awestruck by my predecessors in this field. Not that we do not have good learners in this era, but to me, the builders of yore seem to belong to a different breed of engineer altogether.

We have to acknowledge that these tools themselves have seen rapid development, for example "agentic programming". I recently tried a tool that touts itself as an "agent-first IDE" and was surprised by how well it can organise itself to create a coherent project. A few days ago, I witnessed hackathon organisers creating a website to score the contestants on the spot with an online vibe coding tool. It is a fair assumption that these tools are far better for the paid tier users.

That train of thought eventually led me to this question: What do LLM service providers stand to gain from providing all of this for free? The first thing that comes to mind is that they want people to buy their paid services. And the immediate next thing that follows is that they want the users to become dependent on these LLMs to sustain their business model.

I will never argue that LLMs are bad, but I will argue that the way we are trying to use in in everyday life is not the best way to do it. Personally, I really like using them as search engines, and ask it to return the links to the sites it finds. It is common knowledge that search engines are not as good as they used to be, so this application of LLMs is something that actually amplifies and augments my usage of the internet.

On the flip side, it has enabled people to generate an ungracious amount of code. This is in extension a rant about how JavaScript is used in places that it has no business being. Well, we will see how this situation turns out. I've heard an interviewer telling me they pushed 60 thousand lines of code in 5 months. Is that rate really sustainable? Time will tell.

For now, let this site stand as a modern relic, as remembrance for the simpler times on the internet we lost, and as hope to break free of the curse that has befallen the modern internet.
